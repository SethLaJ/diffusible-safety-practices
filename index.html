Rogers Innovation Scoring — 75 Road Safety Practices

Seth LaJeunesse, UNC Highway Safety Research Center  ·  Analysis developed with Claude (Anthropic)

Why do some proven road safety practices spread quickly while others stall for decades despite strong evidence? Sociologist Everett Rogers argued in his landmark work Diffusion of Innovations that the answer lies less in the quality of the evidence and more in how practitioners perceive the practice itself. This tool applies Rogers’ five adoption attributes to 75 road safety practices, scored from the perspective of a U.S. road safety practitioner — a multi-sector population spanning transportation engineering, planning, public health, law enforcement, and post-crash care. It also incorporates empirical practitioner appraisals of feasibility and impact drawn from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2025), NCHRP Report 1135, in which professionals across these sectors rated each practice on standardized scales. Where a practice was rated by multiple professional sectors, scores are averaged across sectors. Click any row to see full scoring rationale and NCHRP data.

Relative advantage
Is this practice perceived as meaningfully better than what it replaces? Stronger perceived benefit — in safety outcomes, liability protection, or professional recognition — accelerates adoption.
Trialability
Can practitioners test this on a small scale before committing fully? Reversible pilots lower perceived risk. Practices requiring system-wide rollout from day one spread more slowly.
Observability
Are the results visible to peers? Clear, measurable before-after outcomes that travel through professional networks generate social proof for subsequent adopters.
Compatibility
Does this fit existing professional values, standards, and workflows? Practices conflicting with federal standards, requiring inter-agency authority, or demanding a paradigm shift face steeper curves.
Low complexity
How easy is this to understand and implement? Score is inverted — 5 = low complexity = favorable. Practices requiring new training, software, or multi-agency coordination score lower.
Only Rogers attribute where higher = simpler = easier to adopt.
NCHRP feasibility (Z-score)
Standardized score from the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2025), NCHRP Report 1135. Practitioners rated financial, social, and political feasibility. Z > 0 = above average feasibility; Z < 0 = below average. Averaged across sectors where multiple groups rated the same practice.
NCHRP impact (Z-score)
Practitioners rated the practice’s ability to improve safety. Z > 0 = above average perceived impact; Z < 0 = below average. Averaged across sectors where applicable. High feasibility + high impact = strongest diffusion candidates.
Scoring methodology  ·  Rogers attributes scored 1–5 from the perspective of a U.S. road safety practitioner (multi-sector). NCHRP Z-scores are empirical practitioner ratings from NCHRP Report 1135 (National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, 2025), standardized within each appendix (policy, planning, design, operations, enforcement, post-crash). Rogers composite out of 25 determines prioritization tier: Prioritize first (≥20)  ·  Prioritize second (15–19)  ·  Longer horizon (≤14). “Longer horizon” practices often require a different adopter population or institutional runway rather than being low priority.
