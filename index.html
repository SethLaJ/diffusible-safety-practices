Rogers Innovation Scoring — 75 Road Safety Practices

Seth LaJeunesse, UNC Highway Safety Research Center  ·  Analysis developed with Claude (Anthropic)

Why do some proven road safety practices spread quickly while others stall for decades despite strong evidence? Sociologist Everett Rogers argued in his landmark work Diffusion of Innovations that the answer lies less in the quality of the evidence and more in how practitioners perceive the practice itself. This tool applies Rogers’ five adoption attributes to 75 road safety practices, scored from the perspective of a mid-size city department of transportation engineer with Vision Zero exposure — the professional population most strategically positioned to seed transformational safety practice in the United States. Use the filters and search below to explore which practices are most diffusion-ready, and why.

Relative advantage
Is this practice perceived as meaningfully better than what it replaces? The stronger the perceived benefit — in safety outcomes, liability protection, or professional recognition — the faster adoption tends to be.
Trialability
Can a practitioner test this on a small scale before committing fully? Reversible pilots lower the perceived risk of adoption. Practices that require city-wide rollout from day one spread more slowly.
Observability
Are the results visible to peers? Practices with clear, measurable before-after outcomes that can be shared with colleagues generate social proof. Abstract or long-lag outcomes are harder to communicate.
Compatibility
Does this fit existing professional values, standards, and workflows? Practices that clash with AASHTO standards, require inter-agency authority, or demand a paradigm shift face steeper adoption curves.
Low complexity
How easy is this to understand and implement? Score is inverted — a 5 means low complexity (favorable). Practices requiring new technical training, software, or multi-agency coordination score lower.
Only Rogers attribute where higher = simpler = easier to adopt.
Scoring methodology  ·  Each practice is scored 1–5 on each attribute from the perspective of a mid-size city DOT engineer in the NC Triangle Vision Zero cluster. Scores reflect perceived attributes, not objective properties of the practice. Composite scores out of 25 determine priority tier: Seed first (≥ 20)  ·  Seed second (15–19)  ·  Longer horizon (≤ 14). Practices in the “longer horizon” tier are not unimportant — they require a different adopter population (state legislature, NHTSA, city planners) or a longer institutional runway.
