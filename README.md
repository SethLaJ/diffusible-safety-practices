# Diffusible Safety Practices

An interactive scoring tool that applies Everett Rogers' *Diffusion of Innovations* 
framework to 75 road safety practices, evaluating each across five adoption attributes: 
relative advantage, trialability, observability, compatibility, and complexity.

Practices are scored from the perspective of a **U.S. road safety practitioner** — 
a multi-sector population spanning transportation engineering, planning, public health, 
law enforcement, and post-crash care.

The tool also incorporates practitioner appraisals of **feasibility** and 
**impact** drawn from *NCHRP Report 1135*, in which professionals across these sectors 
rated each practice on standardized scales. Where a practice was rated by multiple 
professional sectors, scores are averaged across sectors.

## Use the tool

[Open the interactive explorer →](https://sethlaj.github.io/diffusible-safety-practices/)

## What the tool shows

Each practice is scored 1–5 on all five of Rogers' attributes. Complexity is inverted 
(5 = low complexity = favorable for adoption). The composite score out of 25 
determines priority tier:

- **Prioritize first** — score ≥ 20
- **Prioritize second** — score 15–19
- **Longer horizon** — score ≤ 14

Practices in the "longer horizon" tier are not unimportant — they require a different 
adopter population (e.g., state legislature, NHTSA, city planners) or a longer institutional 
runway. Click any row in the tool to see the full scoring rationale and NCHRP 
practitioner data for that practice.

## Sources

**Rogers scores:** LaJeunesse & Claude (Anthropic), 2025. Adopter population: U.S. 
road safety practitioner (multi-sector).

**NCHRP practitioner ratings:** National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and 
Medicine. (2025). *A Guide to Applying the Safe System Approach to Transportation 
Planning, Design, and Operations.* Washington, DC: The National Academies Press. 
doi: 10.17226/29148

**Rogers framework:** Rogers, E.M. (2003). *Diffusion of Innovations* (5th ed.). 
Free Press.
